## Step 1: Define What You Want Your Website to Achieve

Before choosing platforms or colors, answer these three questions:

- **What do you want people to do on your site?** Call you, book an appointment, buy directly, view your catalog.
- **What information does your customer need to make a decision?** Prices, location, hours, examples of your work, testimonials.
- **What sets you apart from the competition?** Your story, your way of working, your guarantee, your specialty.

With those answers clear, everything else gets easier. Your website doesn't need to be the prettiest one out there — it needs to answer what your customer is looking for.

## Step 2: Get Your Domain and Hosting

Your **domain** is your website's address (e.g., yourbusiness.com). Your **hosting** is where your website lives (the server where it's stored).

**Domain:** Costs between $10 and $15 USD per year. Get it from Namecheap, GoDaddy, or your hosting provider.

Tips for choosing a domain:

- Use .com if available. If not, .co or .net are good alternatives.
- Keep it short, easy to say and spell.
- Avoid numbers and hyphens (e.g., "your-business-1.com" is hard to remember).
- Use your business name if it's registered. No need to get creative with the domain.

**Hosting:** For a small business, shared hosting or modern platforms (like the ones we use at Tp3studio) work perfectly. You don't need a dedicated server or anything expensive.

## Step 3: Choose How to Build Your Website

Here are three paths. Each one suits a different profile.

### Option A: Hire a Professional (Recommended If Your Time Is Valuable)

You pay someone (like us at Tp3studio) to build your site. You just provide the information and the money; they do everything.

**Pros:**
- You don't touch anything technical
- It's ready in 48 hours
- Includes design, domain, hosting, and chatbot
- If something breaks, they fix it

**Cons:**
- Costs money upfront (from $100 USD at Tp3studio)
- You depend on them for major changes

**Who it's for:** Business owners who prefer spending their time serving customers, not learning tech.

### Option B: Use a Visual Builder (Wix, Squarespace, Shopify)

Platforms with templates where you drag and drop elements. You can have something functional in a couple of days if you put in the time.

**Pros:**
- You control everything
- You can work at your own pace
- Plans from $15-$30 USD/month

**Cons:**
- The learning curve is real (you'll be slow the first few hours)
- Drag-and-drop designs look generic
- Monthly costs add up
- No one to help when something goes wrong

**Who it's for:** Entrepreneurs with time, patience, and a desire to learn something new.

### Option C: Custom Code (Not Recommended for Most)

Hiring a developer to build a custom site. Can cost between $500 and $5,000 USD.

**Pros:**
- Total control over every detail
- Scalable if your business grows a lot

**Cons:**
- Very expensive for small businesses
- Takes weeks or months
- If the developer leaves, no one else knows how it works

**Who it's for:** Companies with big budgets and very specific needs.

## Step 4: Build Your Website Content

This is what takes people the longest, and it shouldn't. A small business website only needs these pages:

### Home (one page)
- What you do
- Who you do it for
- Why they should choose you
- A button that says "Contact Me" or "Book Now"

### Services or products
- What you sell
- How much it costs
- What's included

### About you / About your business
- Who you are
- Your story in 3 paragraphs
- A photo of you or your team

### Contact
- Form, WhatsApp, address, phone number
- Map if you have a physical location

### (Optional) Blog or FAQ
- Frequently asked questions
- Articles about what you do

**Content tips:**

- Use real photos of your business, not stock images. An owner with their product is worth more than any generic photo.
- Write like you talk. Nobody wants to read a cold corporate website. "Hi, I'm Maria and I bake cakes" works better than "We are an artisanal bakery with 10 years of experience."
- Make your WhatsApp number visible. In Latin America, people prefer to message rather than call.

## Step 5: Publish and Get Found

Once your site is ready, people need to see it. The three most important channels to start with:

### Google (Free)
Create a **Google Business Profile** and verify your business. That way, when someone searches for "your business in your city," you show up on the map and in search results.

### Social Media
Put your website link in your Instagram, Facebook, and TikTok bios. End every post with "More info at the link in my bio" or "Book directly from my website."

### WhatsApp
Put your website link in your WhatsApp Business description. When someone asks about a product, say "All the prices are on the website — take a look and let me know."
